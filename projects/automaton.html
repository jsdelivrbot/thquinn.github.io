Subset Sum Automata

Subset sum automata are a family of cellular automata based on the subset sum problem. They're on a square grid like Conway's Game of Life, but instead of each cell being just on or off, the state is an integer:

An automaton in this family is defined by three integer parameters: a target offset, a reward, and a penalty. At each time step, each cell determines its target number by adding the target offset to its own value and checks its orthogonal and diagonal neighbors to see if any subset of them sum to this number. If such a subset exists, the reward is added to the cell. If not, the penalty is subtracted from the cell. The minimum allowed value for a cell is 0.

The above example comes from the +3/1/1 automaton, meaning the target offset is +3, the reward is 1, and the penalty is 1. Some example checks:

The target number for this cell is 14 (11+3). There's exactly one subset of its neighbors which sum to 14 — the two 7s — but that's enough for it to earn its reward. This cell's value will increase by the reward (1) to 12 next generation.

The target for this cell is 15 (12+3), and there are four subsets of its neighbors that sum to 15: each of the 15s by themselves. This cell's value will increase by the reward (1) to 13 next generation.

The target for this cell is 16 (13+3) and, although there are subsets of its neighbors that sum to 15 and 17, there's no subset that sums to 16. This cell's value will decrease by the penalty (1) to 12 next generation.

Many members of the subset sum automata have this chaotic, pulsating appearance. Clusters of low values tend to increase, since it's easy to sum them together to whatever target number you're trying to reach, and clusters of high values tend to decrease because they don't have access to smaller neighbors that get them to the exact value they need. In +3/1/1, the penalty is low enough that even when the values of the fan-like structures become too large and they begin to collapse, they stick around until the low-value medium around them aligns in such a way that they can grow again.

+3/1/1 doesn't look Life-like: there are no still lifes, no oscillators, and no spaceships. It's impossible to have a still life when cells are being either rewarded or penalized every turn; oscillators, though, we can do. Here's a period-4 one in +5/4/7:

Between the Life-like and the un-Life-like, plenty of variety can be found just by fiddling with the target offset, penalty, and reward:

+9/5/4: blocky gradients that become less and less diverse until they ultimately die.
+1/7/6: a dense system of long-tailed gliders.
+2/5/8: striated spacefilling regions surrounded by higher values that consume them.
+3/1/∞: a system of octagonal whorls that eventually stabilizes. A large-ish number can stand in for infinity.
+4/3/4: seeded with a 2x2 square of 2s, creates an expanding mandala-like pattern. Values stay within [0,8] with the occasional 11 for the first 226 generations, when finally two 11s have each other and a 4 as neighbors, beginning a cascade of higher numbers.
+3/5/7: a mixture of elementary replicators, gliders, and divergent "still lifes."

You tend to see the most interesting results when the reward and penalty are coprime; this way any cell can reach any value. For example, a rule with a reward of 2 and a penalty of 4 could never see odd values become even or odd values become even. On the subject of undesirable parameters, it's also easy to create rules that tend towards divergent patterns that, while cool looking in their own way, are really just numbers getting larger in larger in a predictable way. A rule with a higher reward than penalty will tend to diverge (but not always, as a couple rules above show). In addition, a low target offset increases the odds of divergence. Unlike their plane-devouring brethren, these bounded divergent clusters can be extremely useful for pattern construction. They're nearly impossible to disrupt and their values are only ever useful for summing to their own target numbers, meaning that as far as their neighbors are concerned, they might as well be locked at 0.
